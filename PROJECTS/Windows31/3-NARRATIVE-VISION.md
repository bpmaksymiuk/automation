## OVERVIEW
This product is a browser-native Windows 3.1-inspired desktop that makes a classic operating environment feel immediate, tactile, and emotionally legible again. It is not trying to imitate a modern app launcher; it is trying to preserve the pleasure of opening a program, moving a small window, and discovering that the system itself is part of the experience. The approved use cases point to a shell-first product where the desktop is the world, the bundled apps are the attractions, and the hidden details are the reward for looking around.

The target audience includes people who lived through the era and want recognition, people discovering retro computing for the first time, and anyone who enjoys small interactive systems with strong personality. The product solves a simple experience problem: most retro web emulations feel either too technical or too static. This one should feel like a place you can inhabit for a few minutes, learn quickly, and remember because it behaves like a tiny operating system rather than a page.

The narrative center is the desktop itself. Booting, launching apps, and uncovering the easter egg are all parts of the same story: a user enters a compact digital room, learns its logic, and leaves with a sense of playful mastery.

## COMPETITIVE & CREATIVE RESEARCH
Windows 3.1 establishes the baseline: a shell-driven system centered on Program Manager and File Manager, with bundled utilities and games that made the desktop feel like a complete environment rather than a single app. That suggests the product should present itself as a coherent system, not a themed website. Source material on Windows 3.1 also reinforces the value of small, clear interactions and the importance of built-in accessories like Notepad and Minesweeper.

Windows93 shows how a retro web OS can lean into personality, hidden jokes, and a slightly surreal sense of discovery. The lesson is that nostalgia gets stronger when the system feels like it has secrets. The opportunity here is to keep that delight while grounding it in a more historically recognizable Windows 3.1 visual language.

PCjs and v86 both demonstrate that browser-based access to old computing environments is viable and compelling. They are strong on preservation and technical reach, but they feel closer to emulation infrastructure than to a polished emotional product. This project should borrow their browser-native accessibility while making the first-time experience simpler, friendlier, and more curated.

ToastyTech's Windows 3.1 gallery is a useful visual and behavioral reference because it captures the look of Program Manager, File Manager, Control Panel, and the window chrome with very little ambiguity. The lesson is that authenticity lives in details: window borders, icon grouping, list layouts, and the way a desktop explains itself without modern onboarding. Source: ToastyTech's gallery pages and screenshots are especially useful for preserving that feel.

## THEMES AND TONE
- **Nostalgic familiarity**: the product should trigger recognition even when the user is not looking for it.
- **Tactile smallness**: everything should feel like a compact object you can move, open, and close.
- **Curated discovery**: the shell should reward exploration without forcing it.
- **Playful restraint**: the product can be charming and surprising, but it should not turn into parody.
- **Historical clarity**: users should always understand what era they are in and why the interface behaves the way it does.

The voice should be warm, clear, and lightly reverent, with enough wit to keep the experience from becoming a museum label. It should read like a place that remembers its own history and is happy to let visitors poke around.

## WORLD-BUILDING / CONCEPTS
- **The desktop as a room**: the shell is not a menu system; it is a room with objects in it.
- **Apps as artifacts**: Notepad, Paint, Calculator, and Minesweeper are not just tools; they are period objects with a use and a mood.
- **The boot sequence as a curtain rise**: startup is a short ritual that frames the experience and makes entry feel intentional.
- **The hidden room**: the easter egg is a secret place that confirms the desktop is larger than it first appeared.
- **Mobile as a compressed version of the same room**: the same world must shrink gracefully onto a touch screen without losing its identity.

These concepts imply a product where navigation is experiential rather than informational. Clicking an icon is like opening a small door. Closing a window is like stepping back into the room. Discovering a hidden interaction is like finding a drawer nobody mentioned.
